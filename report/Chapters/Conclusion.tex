\lhead{Conclusion and Future Work}
\section{Conclusion and Future Research Directions}
\label{SecConclusion}

\subsection{Summary of Findings}
\label{Conc:Summary}

This project investigated how a repair mechanism affects the quality,
diversity, and convergence of a Genetic Algorithm applied to the Traveling
Salesman Problem. The study used the kroA100 benchmark instance (100 cities,
known optimal tour length 21,282) with a complete GA implementation
supporting multiple selection, crossover, and mutation operators and a
configurable repair mechanism with three insertion strategies.

The main findings are as follows:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Repair is necessary for naive crossover.}
    Naive single-point crossover produces offspring with approximately 23
    duplicate cities and 23 missing cities out of 100. Without repair, these
    infeasible chromosomes receive artificially low fitness values because
    they visit fewer unique cities, corrupting selection pressure and steering
    the search toward invalid solutions (\sref{T3:RepairCritique}).

    \item \textbf{Repair outperforms penalty-based constraint handling.}
    In the three-arm comparative experiment (repair, penalty, no-repair),
    repair produced valid tours with a mean best fitness of 49,778,
    significantly lower than the penalty arm's 57,948 ($p < 0.001$,
    Wilcoxon signed-rank test with Bonferroni correction). Direct feasibility
    enforcement is more effective than indirect penalty-based steering for
    this problem instance (\sref{T4:Boxplots}).

    \item \textbf{Repair does not accelerate diversity loss.}
    The repair arm retained slightly higher final population diversity
    (0.013) than the no-repair baseline (0.010) after 500 generations.
    The random-insertion repair strategy introduces stochastic variation
    at repaired positions, counteracting the concern that repair might
    reduce exploration (\sref{T4:Discussion}).

    \item \textbf{Selection method dominates parameter sensitivity.}
    Among the four swept parameters, tournament selection had the largest
    effect on solution quality (median 63,674 vs.\ 89,059 for roulette),
    followed by mutation rate and population size. Crossover rate had the
    smallest effect (\sref{T3:Sensitivity}).

    \item \textbf{Best GA performance.}
    The best configuration (population size 200, crossover rate 0.95,
    mutation rate 0.10, tournament selection, PMX with repair) achieved a
    mean best fitness of 47,539 across 30 seeds, with the best single run
    reaching 37,956 --- a gap of 78.3\% from the known optimal
    (\sref{T3:Performance}).
\end{enumerate}

\subsection{Limitations}
\label{Conc:Limitations}

Several limitations should be noted when interpreting these results.

\textbf{Single benchmark instance.}
All experiments were conducted on kroA100. While this is a standard TSPLIB
benchmark, the findings may not generalise to instances with different
structures (e.g.\ clustered cities, asymmetric distances, or substantially
larger dimensions).

\textbf{No local search.}
The GA used in this project is a plain generational algorithm without
local search operators such as 2-opt or Lin--Kernighan moves. The best
tour found (37,956) is 78.3\% above the known optimal, a gap that local
search could substantially reduce. The project's purpose was to study
constraint-handling strategies rather than to minimise the optimality gap.

\textbf{Single repair strategy tested empirically.}
Although three repair insertion strategies were implemented (random,
positional, nearest-neighbour), only random insertion was used in the
experiments. The other strategies may produce different quality--diversity
trade-offs.

\textbf{Fixed generation budget.}
All runs terminated after 500 generations. Longer runs or adaptive
termination criteria might reveal different convergence behaviours,
particularly for configurations that had not yet plateaued.

\textbf{Penalty weight not tuned.}
The penalty function used a fixed weight of 50,000. A systematic tuning
of the penalty weight might improve the penalty arm's performance,
narrowing or widening the gap with the repair arm.

\subsection{Future Research Directions}
\label{Conc:FutureWork}

The following directions extend naturally from the current work:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Memetic algorithms (hybridisation with local search).}
    Combining the GA with 2-opt or Lin--Kernighan local search after each
    generation would target the main performance limitation of the current
    implementation. The interaction between repair and local search is an
    open question: repair restores feasibility at the chromosome level,
    while local search improves tour quality at the edge level, and their
    combined effect on diversity warrants investigation.

    \item \textbf{Adaptive operator selection.}
    Rather than fixing crossover, mutation, and repair strategies for an
    entire run, adaptive methods could select operators based on their
    recent contribution to fitness improvement. This would allow the GA
    to shift from exploratory operators in early generations to
    exploitative ones as the population converges.

    \item \textbf{Comparison of repair insertion strategies.}
    The three implemented strategies (random, positional, nearest-neighbour)
    offer different trade-offs between speed, bias, and local tour quality.
    A controlled comparison across all three, with diversity and convergence
    tracking, would clarify when each strategy is most appropriate.

    \item \textbf{Scaling to larger instances.}
    Testing on TSPLIB instances with 200--500 cities would reveal whether
    the observed parameter sensitivities and repair effectiveness hold at
    larger scales, where the search space grows factorially and diversity
    maintenance becomes more critical.

    \item \textbf{Multi-objective formulations.}
    Extending the GA to optimise tour length and diversity simultaneously
    (e.g.\ using NSGA-II) could provide a more principled approach to the
    exploration--exploitation trade-off, replacing the implicit diversity
    management of repair with an explicit objective.
\end{enumerate}
