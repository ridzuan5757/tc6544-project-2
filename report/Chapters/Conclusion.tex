\section{Summary of Findings}
\label{Conc:Summary}

We investigated a Genetic Algorithm for the Traveling Salesman Problem on the
kroA100 benchmark (100 cities, known optimal tour length 21\,282). The primary
pipeline pairs naive single-point crossover with a random-insertion repair
step. We compared it against two alternatives: a penalty function and the
feasibility-preserving PMX operator.

The main findings are:

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Repair and PMX are competitive; penalty lags behind.}
    In the three-arm comparison (\sref{T4:Boxplots}), repair achieved a
    mean best fitness of $49\,778$ and PMX $50\,655$, practically tied
    given standard deviations near 3,200--3,600. The penalty arm came in
    noticeably worse at $57\,948$. The repair-centric pipeline is therefore
    a credible alternative to a feasibility-preserving operator, while
    penalty-based handling trails both.

    \item \textbf{Selection method is the dominant parameter, followed by
    mutation rate.}
    The $1\,620$-run sensitivity sweep (\sref{T3:Sensitivity}) shows
    tournament selection strongly outperforming roulette across all
    settings. Higher mutation rates reduce premature convergence and
    improve mean tour length, while population size has a smaller monotonic
    effect. Crossover rate had the weakest standalone effect.

    \item \textbf{Best configuration.}
    Population size $200$, crossover rate $0.95$, mutation rate $0.10$,
    tournament selection achieved a mean best fitness of $49\,347$ (gap
    $131.9\%$ from optimal), with the best single seed reaching $42\,623$
    (gap $100.3\%$).
\end{enumerate}

\section{Limitations}
\label{Conc:Limitations}

\begin{itemize}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Single benchmark instance.} All experiments used kroA100.
    The findings may not generalise to larger instances, asymmetric TSP
    variants, or different city distributions.

    \item \textbf{No local-search hybridisation.} The $\sim\!100\%$ gap
    from optimal reflects the absence of 2-opt or Lin--Kernighan refinement,
    not a flaw in repair itself. A plain generational GA is not expected to
    close this gap on a 100-city instance.

    \item \textbf{Single repair strategy tested.} We implemented three
    insertion strategies (random, positional, nearest-neighbour) but only
    exercised random insertion in the reported experiments.
\end{itemize}

\section{Future Research Directions}
\label{Conc:FutureWork}

\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Hybrid local search.}
    Integrate 2-opt or Lin--Kernighan refinement after crossover and
    repair. This directly targets the optimality gap and is the standard
    route to competitive GA-based TSP results
    (\cite{liu2014powerfulga}).

    \item \textbf{Larger and structurally different benchmarks.}
    Extend the experiments to kroA200, kroA500, and an asymmetric TSP
    instance to test whether the repair $\succ$ PMX $\succ$ penalty
    ranking holds at larger scales.

    \item \textbf{Adaptive repair strategy.}
    Compare random, positional, and nearest-neighbour insertion across the
    same seeds, then design an adaptive variant that selects a rule based
    on current population diversity.

    \item \textbf{Adaptive penalty weights.}
    Implement the violation-factor approach from
    \cite{chehouri2016violationfactor} to determine whether the penalty
    arm's poor showing is intrinsic or simply due to an untuned weight.
\end{enumerate}
