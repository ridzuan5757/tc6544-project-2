\lhead{Introduction}
\section{Introduction}
\label{SecIntroduction}

The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) asks for the shortest closed tour that
visits every city in a given set exactly once before returning to the
starting city. Although the statement is short, the TSP is
NP-hard~\cite{applegate2006traveling}, and its search space grows
factorially with the number of cities, so exhaustive enumeration is
impractical for all but the smallest instances. The TSP has therefore
become a standard testbed for combinatorial metaheuristics, and Genetic
Algorithms (GAs) are a frequently studied
choice~\cite{holland1975adaptation,goldberg1989genetic}. This project
implements a GA for the static single-objective TSP, with explicit
attention to how constraint violations are detected and corrected during
the search.

\subsection{Background}
\label{Intro:Background}

A tour in the TSP is a permutation of the city indices, and a permutation
is feasible only when every city appears exactly once. This requirement
makes operator design unusually consequential: ordinary string-crossover
operators borrowed from binary or real-valued GAs do not preserve the
permutation property, and they routinely produce offspring that contain
duplicated cities and miss other cities. Specialised crossover operators
have been developed precisely for this reason, including Partially Mapped
Crossover (PMX)~\cite{goldberg1985alleles}, Order Crossover
(OX)~\cite{davis1985applying}, and Cycle Crossover
(CX)~\cite{oliver1987study}. When such operators are not used, the GA
must instead handle infeasibility either by penalising it in the fitness
function or by repairing offspring before they are evaluated.

The benchmark instance for this project is kroA100 from
TSPLIB~\cite{reinelt1991tsplib}: 100 cities in the Euclidean plane with
a known optimal tour length of 21\,282. Its size is large enough to
expose differences between constraint-handling strategies, but small
enough that thirty-seed comparisons and parameter sweeps remain
computationally tractable.

\subsection{Problem Statement}
\label{Intro:ProblemStatement}

When a GA for the TSP uses a crossover operator that does not preserve
permutations --- single-point crossover being the simplest example ---
offspring will typically contain duplicated cities at some positions and
omit other cities entirely. Computing tour length over such a chromosome
yields a distance that does not correspond to any valid tour, and the
value is often artificially low because the missing cities never
contribute to the sum. Selection then favours these infeasible
chromosomes, distorting the search and pulling the population away from
the feasible region.

Three constraint-handling strategies address this pathology. Feasibility
can be preserved by construction with permutation-aware operators such
as PMX. Infeasibility can be tolerated but discouraged through a
penalty term proportional to the violation. Infeasibility can also be
removed after recombination by a repair step that detects duplicates
and missing cities and rewrites the chromosome into a valid permutation.
The central question of this project is how these three strategies
compare on a fixed benchmark when the rest of the GA is held constant.

\subsection{Contributions}
\label{Intro:Contributions}

This report makes four contributions. First, it implements a complete
GA pipeline for the TSP using permutation encoding, tournament selection
($k=3$), swap mutation, and elitism with two preserved individuals; the
primary configuration pairs naive single-point crossover with a
random-insertion repair operator that is invoked on every offspring of
every generation, so repair is load-bearing rather than incidental.
Second, it conducts a three-arm comparison on kroA100 over 30
independent seeds, contrasting repair against a penalty function with
weight 50\,000 and against PMX as a feasibility-preserving operator;
the mean tour lengths are 49\,778 for repair, 50\,655 for PMX, and
57\,948 for the penalty function, indicating that direct feasibility
enforcement via repair matches or slightly outperforms a
feasibility-preserving operator on this benchmark and clearly
outperforms a penalty-based alternative. Third, it executes a
1\,620-run parameter sensitivity sweep over population size, crossover
rate, mutation rate, and selection method, identifying a best
configuration of population 200, crossover rate 0.95, mutation rate
0.1, and tournament selection, with mean tour length 49\,347 and an
optimality gap of 131.9\,\%. Fourth, it reports the best tour found
across the study (length 42\,623, gap 100.3\,\%) and provides
convergence and diversity visualisations that support a critical
discussion of where the GA succeeds and where it leaves quality on the
table.
