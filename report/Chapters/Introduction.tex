\lhead{Introduction}
\chapter{Introduction}
\label{SecIntroduction}

The Traveling Salesman Problem (TSP) asks for the shortest closed tour that
visits every city in a given set exactly once before returning to the
starting city. The statement is simple, but the TSP is
NP-hard~\cite{applegate2006traveling} and its search space grows
factorially with the number of cities. Exhaustive enumeration is therefore
impractical for all but the smallest instances. The TSP has long served as
a standard testbed for combinatorial metaheuristics, and Genetic Algorithms
(GAs) are among the most frequently studied
approaches~\cite{holland1975adaptation,goldberg1989genetic}. In this
report we implement a GA for the static single-objective TSP, paying
close attention to how constraint violations are detected and corrected
during the search.

\section{Background}
\label{Intro:Background}

A tour in the TSP is a permutation of the city indices, and a permutation
is feasible only when every city appears exactly once. This makes operator
design unusually important: ordinary crossover operators borrowed from
binary or real-valued GAs do not preserve the permutation property and
routinely produce offspring containing duplicated cities while omitting
others. Specialised operators have been developed for exactly this reason,
including Partially Mapped Crossover (PMX)~\cite{goldberg1985alleles},
Order Crossover (OX)~\cite{davis1985applying}, and Cycle Crossover
(CX)~\cite{oliver1987study}. When such operators are not used, the GA
must handle infeasibility some other way, either by penalising it in
the fitness function or by repairing offspring before evaluation.

We use kroA100 from TSPLIB~\cite{reinelt1991tsplib} as our benchmark:
100 cities in the Euclidean plane with a known optimal tour length of
21\,282. It is large enough to expose meaningful differences between
constraint-handling strategies yet small enough that thirty-seed
comparisons and parameter sweeps stay computationally tractable.

\section{Problem Statement}
\label{Intro:ProblemStatement}

When a GA for the TSP uses a crossover operator that does not preserve
permutations (single-point crossover being the simplest case)
offspring will typically contain duplicated cities at some positions and
omit other cities entirely. Computing tour length on such a chromosome
gives a distance that does not correspond to any valid tour. Worse, the
value is often artificially low because the missing cities never add to
the sum. Selection then favours these infeasible chromosomes, pulling the
population away from the feasible region.

Three strategies address this. First, feasibility can be preserved by
construction using permutation-aware operators such as PMX. Second,
infeasibility can be tolerated but discouraged through a penalty term
proportional to the violation count. Third, infeasibility can be removed
after recombination by a repair step that detects duplicates and missing
cities and rewrites the chromosome into a valid permutation. We want to
know how these three strategies compare on a fixed benchmark when the
rest of the GA pipeline is held constant.

\section{Contributions}
\label{Intro:Contributions}

We implement a full GA pipeline for the TSP using permutation encoding,
tournament selection ($k=3$), swap mutation, and elitism. The primary
configuration pairs naive single-point crossover with a random-insertion
repair operator that is active on every generation, so every offspring is
guaranteed feasible before evaluation. We then run a three-arm comparison
on kroA100 over 30 seeds, contrasting repair against a penalty function
and against PMX. Repair and PMX produce similar mean tour lengths while
penalty trails both by a clear margin. A 1\,620-run parameter sweep
supplements the comparison, and we report convergence curves, diversity
traces, and tour plots alongside a critical discussion of where the GA
works well and where it falls short.
