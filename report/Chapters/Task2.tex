\lhead{Task 2. \emph{Algorithm Development}}
\chapter{Task 2: Algorithm Development}
\label{Task2}

This chapter specifies the genetic algorithm and the repair mechanism that
handles the permutation constraint. \sref{T2:GADesign} fixes the
representation and the five evolutionary operators. \sref{T2:Repair} explains
why the primary recombination operator produces infeasible offspring every
generation and describes the repair step that converts them back into valid
Hamiltonian tours. The empirical comparison of repair against alternative
constraint-handling approaches is reported in \sref{T3:RepairCritique}.

\section{Genetic Algorithm Design (Task 2.1)}
\label{T2:GADesign}

\subsection{Solution Representation}
\label{T2:Representation}
Each candidate solution is encoded as a permutation of the city index set
$\{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\}$. A chromosome is an ordered tuple where the $i$-th
gene names the $i$-th city visited; the tour is closed by returning to the
first gene. For the eight-city illustration used later, the chromosome
$[3,0,7,1,5,2,6,4]$ denotes the tour
$3 \to 0 \to 7 \to 1 \to 5 \to 2 \to 6 \to 4 \to 3$. The fitness of a
chromosome $\pi$ is its closed Euclidean tour length,
\begin{equation}
f(\pi) \;=\; \sum_{i=0}^{n-1} d_{\pi_i,\,\pi_{(i+1) \bmod n}},
\end{equation}
where $d_{ij}$ is the Euclidean distance between cities $i$ and $j$.
Because fitness is computed by indexing the distance matrix at the gene
values, the calculation is only meaningful when the chromosome is a true
permutation. A chromosome with duplicate or missing cities still produces a
number, but that number is not the length of a Hamiltonian tour and must
not be admitted into selection (see \sref{T2:Infeasibility}).

\subsection{Population Initialisation}
\label{T2:Init}
We generate the initial population as $N$ independent random permutations
of the city set, using
\texttt{numpy.random.default\_rng(seed).permutation(n)}. Each chromosome is
sampled independently, so the initial population is drawn uniformly from
the space of $n!$ permutations and starts at near-maximal diversity. We do
not inject any heuristic seeding (such as a nearest-neighbour tour), which
keeps the convergence behaviour reported in \sref{T4:Convergence}
attributable to the evolutionary operators alone. A single seed controls
every stochastic component of the GA, making each run reproducible and
supporting paired-seed comparisons in \sref{T3:Sensitivity}.

\subsection{Selection}
\label{T2:Selection}
We implement two selection mechanisms, exposed through a single
configuration parameter so that the choice of parent-selection rule can be
swept alongside any numerical hyperparameter.

Tournament selection is the primary operator. Given a population $P$ of
size $N$, $k$ candidates are drawn uniformly at random without replacement
and the candidate with the lowest tour length is returned,
\begin{equation}
s \;=\; \arg\min_{i \in C} f(i), \qquad C \subseteq P,\; |C| = k.
\end{equation}
The tournament size $k$ controls selection pressure; small $k$ keeps weak
individuals in play, large $k$ narrows the gene pool quickly. We use
$k = 3$ in the primary configuration, which retains enough randomness to
delay premature convergence while still favouring the better half of the
population. The sweep in \sref{T3:Sensitivity} varies $k$ explicitly.

Roulette wheel selection serves as the comparison operator. Because the TSP
is a minimisation problem, raw fitness cannot be used as a selection weight
directly. Instead we assign each individual the inverse fitness and
normalise to a probability distribution,
\begin{equation}
p_i \;=\; \frac{1/f_i}{\sum_{j=1}^{N} 1/f_j}.
\end{equation}
The roulette draw then picks a parent with probability $p_i$. The same
unified \texttt{select} function dispatches on the
\texttt{selection\_method} field of the GA configuration, so the loop body
is identical for both methods.

\subsection{Crossover}
\label{T2:Crossover}
The primary recombination operator is naive single-point crossover. Given
two parents $A$ and $B$ of length $n$ and a cut point
$pt \in \{1, \ldots, n-1\}$, the child is constructed by concatenation,
\begin{equation}
\mathrm{child} \;=\; A[\,0\,{:}\,pt\,] \,\Vert\, B[\,pt\,{:}\,n\,].
\end{equation}
This operator copies positions independently without checking whether the
inserted gene values already appear elsewhere in the child. It does not
preserve the permutation property: the same city may appear twice and
another may disappear. We chose it deliberately. Pairing naive crossover
with repair means the repair mechanism runs on every offspring, rather than
sitting idle because a permutation-preserving operator already handled
feasibility. This matches the assignment's focus on repair as the central
constraint-handling contribution.

Three feasibility-preserving operators are also implemented for the
comparison in \sref{T3:RepairCritique}. Partially Mapped Crossover (PMX)
of \cite{goldberg1985alleles} copies a segment $A[p_1{:}p_2]$ into the
child and routes each displaced value from $B$ through the segment mapping
until it reaches an unoccupied position; remaining slots are filled
directly from $B$. Order Crossover (OX), due to \cite{davis1985applying}
and refined in \cite{oliver1987study}, also copies the segment from $A$
and then walks $B$ in wrap-around order from just after $p_2$, skipping any
value already present in the segment. Cycle Crossover (CX) discovers
position-wise cycles between the two parents and inherits whole cycles
alternately from each parent, so every position is filled once and every
city appears once. The constructive arguments behind PMX, OX, and CX,
together with comparative reviews of permutation operators, are summarised
in \cite{larranaga1999genetic} and
\cite{cicirello2023permutationoperators}; the worked example in
\sref{T2:Infeasibility} shows the three operators producing valid
permutations on the same parents that defeat naive crossover.

\subsection{Mutation}
\label{T2:Mutation}
Swap mutation is the sole mutation operator in the primary pipeline. On
each invocation we draw a uniform variate from $[0,1)$; if it falls below
the mutation rate $r_m$, two distinct gene positions $i$ and $j$ are
sampled without replacement and their values are exchanged. The chromosome
length, the set of cities, and the permutation property are all preserved
by construction, so swap mutation introduces no infeasibility of its own.
Its perturbation is local --- exactly two edges change --- which suits
late-stage refinement. Inversion mutation, where the segment between two
cut points is reversed in place, is implemented in the code base as a
comparison operator but is not used by any primary experiments in this
report.

\subsection{Main Loop}
We combine the five operators into a generational GA with elitism. At each
generation the top $e$ individuals are copied unchanged into the next
population; the remainder is generated by repeated selection, crossover
(with probability $r_c$), mutation, and (if enabled) repair. We fix the
elitism count at $e = 2$ throughout the primary experiments, which protects
the incumbent best while leaving most of the population open to
recombination. Algorithm \ref{alg:ga} states the loop precisely.

\begin{algorithm}[htbp]
\caption{Generational GA with Optional Repair}
\label{alg:ga}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\State Initialise population $P$ of $N$ random permutations
\State Evaluate fitness $f(p)$ for all $p \in P$
\For{$g = 1$ to $G_{\max}$}
    \State $P' \gets$ top-$e$ elites from $P$ \Comment{elitism}
    \While{$|P'| < N$}
        \State Select parents $p_1, p_2$ from $P$ via tournament or roulette
        \If{$\mathrm{rand}() < r_c$}
            \State $c \gets \mathrm{Crossover}(p_1, p_2)$ \Comment{naive single-point in the primary pipeline}
        \Else
            \State $c \gets \mathrm{copy}(p_1)$
        \EndIf
        \State $c \gets \mathrm{Mutate}(c, r_m)$ \Comment{swap mutation}
        \If{repair enabled}
            \State $c \gets \mathrm{Repair}(c)$
        \EndIf
        \State Append $c$ to $P'$
    \EndWhile
    \State $P \gets P'$; evaluate fitness for all $p \in P$
\EndFor
\State \Return best individual in $P$
\end{algorithmic}
\end{algorithm}

\section{Constraint Handling via Repair Mechanism (Task 2.2)}
\label{T2:Repair}

The TSP imposes a single hard constraint: every city must appear exactly
once. We cannot relax it without changing the problem, so the GA either
uses recombination operators that preserve it by construction or repairs
violations after they occur. Surveys of evolutionary constraint handling
describe the same trichotomy --- preservation, repair, and penalty
\cite{coello2002constraint,michalewicz1996evolutionary}. We take the repair
route, paired with naive single-point crossover so that repair does real
work on every offspring.

\subsection{Why Infeasibility Arises}
\label{T2:Infeasibility}
Naive single-point crossover treats the chromosome like an ordinary
fixed-position string. It copies gene positions independently and does not
check whether the inserted value already appears elsewhere in the child.
For a permutation representation the constraint is global rather than
positional, so this position-by-position copy is exactly the operation that
breaks the city set.

Consider the worked example from
\texttt{notebooks/11\_infeasibility\_analysis.ipynb}. The parents are
\begin{equation*}
A = (0,1,2,3,4,5,6,7), \qquad
B = (2,6,4,0,5,7,1,3),
\end{equation*}
and the single cut point is $pt = 4$. The child constructed by naive
single-point crossover is
\begin{equation*}
\mathrm{child} \;=\; A[\,0{:}4\,] \,\Vert\, B[\,4{:}8\,]
\;=\; (0,1,2,3,5,7,1,3).
\end{equation*}
City $1$ now appears at positions $1$ and $6$, and city $3$ appears at
positions $3$ and $7$. Cities $4$ and $6$ never appear. The chromosome has
the correct length but is not a Hamiltonian tour. The same mechanism causes
the violation reported in the \texttt{12\_repair\_mechanism.ipynb} demo on
kroA100, where every one of thirty naive-crossover offspring was infeasible,
with twenty-four duplicates on average.

This breaks selection. Fitness evaluation on $(0,1,2,3,5,7,1,3)$ still
returns a number, but that number sums distances over an edge set that
visits city $1$ twice, visits city $3$ twice, and skips cities $4$ and $6$
entirely. The result tends to be artificially small because cheap edges
contribute repeatedly while expensive missing-city edges are dropped.
Tournament and roulette selection then favour the infeasible chromosome as
if its fitness were comparable to genuine tour lengths. The invalid genetic
material spreads through recombination, and within a few generations the
population converges toward short pseudo-tours rather than short tours. The
infeasibility analysis in \cite{cicirello2023permutationoperators} traces
the same failure mode across the permutation operator family.

The contrast with permutation-preserving operators makes the mechanism
clear. On the same parents and cut points, PMX returns
$(5,6,2,3,4,7,1,0)$, OX returns $(0,5,2,3,4,7,1,6)$, and CX returns
$(0,6,2,3,4,5,1,7)$; all three are valid permutations because each
operator embeds a feasibility-preserving rule into the copy step itself.
The repair mechanism described below recovers the same guarantee by acting
after a naive copy rather than during one.

\subsection{Repair Design}
\label{T2:RepairDesign}
The repair function follows a two-phase structure: diagnose the infeasible
chromosome, then correct it. The diagnosis phase is a single left-to-right
scan. A \emph{seen} set records the first occurrence of each city in scan
order; any subsequent occurrence of the same city is flagged as a duplicate
position. After the scan, the missing set is computed as
$\{0, 1, \ldots, n-1\} \setminus \mathrm{seen}$. Because the chromosome
length is fixed at $n$, the number of duplicate positions equals the number
of missing cities, so every duplicate can be replaced with a unique missing
city.

The correction phase assigns the missing cities to the duplicate positions.
The order of assignment distinguishes three strategies:
\begin{enumerate}[nosep]
    \item \textbf{Random insertion} (the primary strategy) shuffles the
          missing cities with the GA's seeded random generator and
          places them at the duplicate positions in shuffled order.
    \item \textbf{Position insertion} places the missing cities in
          sorted order at the duplicate positions in left-to-right
          order. The assignment is deterministic, so the same
          infeasible chromosome always repairs to the same tour.
    \item \textbf{Nearest-neighbour insertion} processes the duplicate
          positions left to right and, at each position, chooses the
          still-unassigned missing city whose Euclidean distance to the
          immediately preceding gene in the chromosome is smallest.
\end{enumerate}
We use random insertion as the primary choice. It introduces no bias from
the problem geometry, so the repair step contributes feasibility without
contributing optimisation --- the GA's selection pressure remains the sole
driver of solution quality. Random insertion also preserves population
diversity better than the deterministic alternatives, because two infeasible
chromosomes with the same duplicates and missing cities can still be
repaired into different tours. The feasibility-preserving repair literature
notes the same tension between feasibility and
diversity~\cite{padhye2015feasibilitypreserving}. Algorithm~\ref{alg:ga}
places the repair call after mutation, so any chromosome reaching the
fitness evaluation step at the end of the loop is guaranteed to be a valid
permutation.

The repair function is shown working step by step in the worked example
of \texttt{12\_repair\_mechanism.ipynb}. On the eight-city chromosome
$(3,1,4,1,5,2,6,3)$, the diagnosis phase identifies duplicate positions
$\{3, 7\}$ and missing cities $\{0, 7\}$. Random insertion may return
$(3,1,4,7,5,2,6,0)$; position insertion deterministically returns
$(3,1,4,0,5,2,6,7)$; nearest-neighbour insertion on the toy octagon
returns $(3,1,4,7,5,2,6,0)$. All three are valid permutations. The
notebook's property test runs the repair on five hundred random
corruptions across all three strategies and observes a valid permutation
in every case.

\subsection{Effect on Quality, Diversity, and Convergence}
\label{T2:RepairEffect}
The repair step shapes the GA's behaviour in several ways. We measure these
effects quantitatively in \sref{T3:RepairCritique} and
\sref{T4:Convergence}; here we set expectations.

The most immediate consequence is that every chromosome reaching the
fitness evaluation step in Algorithm~\ref{alg:ga} is a true permutation.
The tour-length number returned by the distance matrix indexing is
therefore the actual length of a Hamiltonian tour over $n$ cities. Without
repair, the same function returns a number corrupted by duplicate and
missing-city counting --- apparent tour lengths look smaller because they
sum over fewer edges, not because the underlying tours are better.
\sref{T3:RepairCritique} confirms this: the no-repair arm posts a lower
mean fitness at generation five hundred, but that fitness does not
correspond to a valid 100-city tour.

Repair also interacts with diversity. Random insertion injects stochastic
variation at the duplicate positions, so a population that would otherwise
lose diversity through repeated crossovers between similar chromosomes
retains some Hamming distance. The diversity trajectory in
\sref{T3:RepairCritique} shows the repair arm holding slightly higher
Hamming distance than the no-repair baseline at the same generation, even
though both arms lose most initial diversity by generation five hundred.
Deterministic repair strategies do not provide this benefit, which is part
of why we chose random insertion over position insertion.

Finally, repair keeps the search inside the feasible region, so the
apparent improvement in best fitness across generations reflects actual
tour-length reduction rather than increasing constraint violation. In
\sref{T3:RepairCritique}, the repair pipeline converges to solutions
comparable with the feasibility-preserving PMX alternative and narrowly
outperforms it on the kroA100 paired-seed runs. The penalty function
comparator, by contrast, shows the failure mode discussed in
\cite{coello2002constraint,michalewicz1996evolutionary}: the penalty weight
is hard to tune, and the GA converges either to overpenalised
feasible-but-poor regions or to underpenalised infeasible regions.
\sref{T3:RepairCritique} reports the magnitudes; we retained repair as
the primary mechanism based on its feasibility guarantee, diversity
preservation, and stable convergence.
