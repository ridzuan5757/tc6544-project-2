\subsection{Parameter Sensitivity Analysis (Task 3.1)}
\label{T3:Sensitivity}

The sensitivity study used a full-factorial sweep on kroA100 with the
repair-based pipeline (naive single-point crossover, swap mutation,
duplicate/missing-city repair on every offspring). The swept parameters and
their levels are population size $\in \{50, 100, 200\}$, crossover rate
$\in \{0.70, 0.85, 0.95\}$, mutation rate $\in \{0.01, 0.05, 0.10\}$, and
selection method $\in \{\text{tournament}, \text{roulette}\}$. The design
yields $3 \times 3 \times 3 \times 2 = 54$ unique configurations. Each
configuration was executed for 30 independent seeds with a fixed budget of
500 generations, producing $54 \times 30 = 1{,}620$ runs. The shared
instance, fitness, and termination settings are listed in
\sref{T1:Setup}.

\tref{tab:sweep-top} reports the five best configurations ranked by mean
best fitness over 30 seeds. The gap column expresses the mean as a
percentage above the known optimal tour length of 21,282.

All five leading configurations use tournament selection at the largest
population size with the two highest mutation rates, which already
foreshadows the marginal-effect ordering below. The per-parameter
distributions in \autoref{fig:boxplot-pop}, \autoref{fig:boxplot-mutation},
and \autoref{fig:boxplot-selection} are computed by collapsing the sweep
over the remaining axes, so each box aggregates the runs in which that
parameter level appears.

\textbf{Selection method.} Tournament selection produces a median final
tour length of 64,894, while roulette-wheel selection produces 86,632.
This is the largest single-parameter effect in the sweep. Roulette
assigns a selection probability proportional to fitness magnitude, and on
this instance the spread of tour lengths in the population (tens of
thousands of units across hundreds of individuals) is small relative to
the absolute magnitudes, so the proportional weights flatten and the
operator behaves close to uniform random selection. Tournament selection
imposes a deterministic best-of-$k$ rule that is invariant to absolute
fitness scale, and it therefore retains strong selection pressure
throughout the run.

\textbf{Mutation rate.} The medians by mutation level are 70,464 at
$r_m = 0.10$, 76,859 at $r_m = 0.05$, and 91,121 at $r_m = 0.01$. This is
the second-largest effect. The repair step is destructive enough that
the population collapses toward a small set of representative tours
within the first 100--200 generations; without enough mutation, the GA
cannot reinject diversity once it has been lost, and the remaining 300+
generations contribute almost nothing. At $r_m = 0.01$ the typical
offspring is essentially identical to its parents after crossover and
repair, so the search stalls at whatever local basin it has reached.

\textbf{Population size.} The medians are 76,355 at pop=200, 81,323 at
pop=100, and 82,998 at pop=50. This is the smallest of the three
discrete effects studied. Larger populations carry more genetic material
into selection, which gives crossover a broader pool of building blocks
to recombine, but the marginal return is modest compared with switching
selection method or raising mutation rate. The cost is roughly linear in
population size for both memory and per-generation time.

Crossover rate $r_c$ varies across the top configurations from 0.70 to
0.95 without a clear ordering, and the sweep table reflects this:
$r_c = 0.95$ wins by a small margin but $r_c = 0.70$ ranks second, ahead
of $r_c = 0.85$. The crossover-rate effect is therefore noise-dominated
at the scale of 30 seeds per cell.

The chosen best configuration is therefore pop\_size~$=$~200,
$r_c$~$=$~0.95, $r_m$~$=$~0.10, tournament selection. This setting is the
top row of \tref{tab:sweep-top} and is used downstream as the best-config
replay analysed in \sref{T4:BestTour}.

\subsection{Performance Evaluation (Task 3.2)}
\label{T3:Performance}

\textbf{Solution quality.} At the chosen best configuration the GA
achieves a mean best tour length of 49,347 with a standard deviation of
3,734, corresponding to a gap of 131.9\% from the known optimum of
21,282. The best single seed reaches 42,623, a gap of 100.3\%. These
absolute gaps are large in the context of a 100-city instance with a
known optimum near 21k. The numbers are nevertheless consistent with
expectations for the operator stack in use: naive single-point crossover
is not feasibility-preserving, the random-insertion repair step restores
validity but injects no problem-specific guidance, and no local-search
component (2-opt, Lin--Kernighan, or similar) is applied. The GA is
therefore searching the permutation space using only structural
recombination and random perturbation, without any TSP-specific heuristic
to refine the surviving tours.

\textbf{Convergence rate.} The convergence curves reproduced in
\sref{T4:Convergence} and \autoref{fig:conv-strategy} show that the bulk
of the improvement happens in the first roughly 200 generations. After
that point the mean best fitness flattens, and the remaining 300
generations contribute only marginal refinement. The 500-generation
termination is therefore set comfortably past the elbow rather than at
it, which gives each run room to consolidate but also explains why the
final means cluster: by generation 500 nearly every run has finished its
visible improvement.

\textbf{Stability across trials.} The standard deviations of the top
configurations sit between roughly 2,600 and 3,800 tour-length units,
i.e.\ about 5--8\% of the mean. The coefficient of variation is moderate
and consistent across the top five rows in \tref{tab:sweep-top}, and the
boxplots in \autoref{fig:boxplot-pop}--\autoref{fig:boxplot-selection}
show no extreme outliers at the chosen best setting. Run-to-run
variability is dominated by where each seed's population converges
within the first 100--200 generations rather than by late-stage
divergence.

\textbf{Computational efficiency.} The full sweep of 1,620 runs at 500
generations each completed in roughly 15 minutes of wall time using 8
worker processes on the development machine, so per-run cost is on the
order of 0.5--1 second. Within a single run the repair step adds $O(n)$
work per offspring, where $n = 100$ is the number of cities, which is
small relative to the $O(n)$ fitness evaluation already performed each
generation. The dominant cost driver is population size: doubling
pop\_size roughly doubles per-generation work, but the marginal solution
quality from pop=100 to pop=200 is modest (see \tref{tab:sweep-top}),
which is the quality--cost trade-off the chosen configuration accepts.

\subsection{Critical Analysis of Repair Mechanism (Task 3.3)}
\label{T3:RepairCritique}

The critical comparison required by the brief uses the three-arm
experiment from TICKET-17. All three arms share the same GA
hyperparameters --- population size 100, crossover rate 0.8, mutation
rate 0.2, tournament selection, swap mutation, 500 generations, 30 seeds
--- so the only varying factor is how the GA handles the feasibility
constraint that each chromosome must be a Hamiltonian permutation of 100
cities.

The three strategies are:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Repair} (the primary pipeline). Naive single-point crossover
    can introduce duplicate cities and missing cities. After crossover and
    mutation the offspring is repaired by random-insertion: duplicates are
    removed and missing cities are inserted at random positions. Every
    evaluated chromosome is a valid tour.
    \item \textbf{Penalty.} Naive single-point crossover with no repair.
    Infeasible chromosomes are evaluated and assigned a penalty of
    $w \cdot v$ added to fitness, where $w = 50{,}000$ is the
    penalty weight and $v$ is the number of constraint violations
    (duplicate or missing cities). Infeasible individuals survive in the
    population but face strong selection pressure against them.
    \item \textbf{PMX (feasibility-preserving).} Partially-mapped crossover
    is used in place of naive crossover. PMX is closed over the set of
    permutations by construction, so no repair or penalty is required.
\end{itemize}

\textbf{Effectiveness of repair.} Repair attains the lowest mean tour
length of the three arms, 49,778 versus 50,655 for PMX and 57,948 for
penalty. The repair--PMX margin is roughly 1.7\% of the mean and is
small compared with the within-arm spread (standard deviations near
3,200--3,600), so the two feasibility-respecting strategies are
practically tied on this instance. Penalty trails both by roughly
16--17\% of the mean, well outside the within-arm spread. The minimum
single-seed values follow the same ordering at the tail: PMX produces
the best individual seed at 42,976 with repair close behind at 43,891,
while penalty's best seed is at 48,092.

\textbf{Trade-offs between feasibility enforcement and exploration.} The
three strategies place the feasibility constraint at different points in
the offspring lifecycle, and each choice trades a different aspect of
search behaviour. Repair enforces feasibility after crossover and
mutation by overwriting the offspring's duplicate positions with the
missing cities at random. This destroys the local genetic information
that the offspring inherited about those positions --- whatever the
crossover operator was trying to combine at the conflict sites is
discarded --- but it guarantees that every evaluated chromosome
contributes a real tour to the next generation's selection pressure. PMX
preserves more parental structure because the crossover operator itself
respects the permutation constraint and never produces conflicts, but
PMX is one specific operator from a constrained family, so the
recombination dynamics it can express are narrower than the wider set of
naive-plus-repair combinations. Penalty preserves all genetic
information from the operators, infeasible or not, but spends part of
the selection budget on candidates that may never become feasible during
the run; the penalty weight then has to be tuned high enough to
discourage infeasibility without flattening the fitness landscape among
feasible candidates.

\textbf{Comparison with alternatives.} Penalty is the weakest of the
three on this instance, trailing repair by about 16\% in mean tour
length. The penalty weight of 50,000 is large enough that infeasible
individuals are clearly disfavoured in tournaments against feasible
ones, but until the population converges away from infeasibility the GA
is still spending part of its selection pressure on chromosomes that
contribute no usable tour information. PMX is the most architecturally
informative comparator because it removes the feasibility problem at the
operator level rather than patching it after the fact. The fact that
repair edges out PMX by only about 2\% indicates that the assignment's
repair-centric pipeline is competitive with a clean
feasibility-preserving alternative on kroA100, and that on this instance
the choice between the two is essentially a design preference rather
than a performance gap. The pairwise differences are visible in the
boxplot spread in \autoref{fig:boxplot-strategy} without requiring
inferential machinery.
