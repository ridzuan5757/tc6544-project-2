\lhead{Task 4. \emph{Visualisation and Results}}
\section{Task 4: Visualisation and Results}
\label{Task4}

This chapter visualises the experimental output from the previous chapter.
Part A is the three-arm constraint-handling comparison (repair vs.\ PMX vs.\
penalty); Part B is the per-parameter sensitivity sweep around the best
configuration. All convergence curves are mean trajectories across 30 seeds;
all boxplots aggregate the 30 final tour lengths from those same runs.

\subsection{Convergence Curves}
\label{T4:Convergence}

\fref{fig:conv-strategy} compares the best-fitness trajectories of the three
constraint-handling strategies. The repair arm drops fastest in the first
50--100 generations and settles to the lowest mean plateau; PMX tracks repair
closely once its built-in feasibility takes effect; the penalty arm stays
visibly higher throughout because the additive penalty increases the
fitness values of partially infeasible parents that selection still
passes on to the next generation.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/figures/convergence_best_fitness.pdf}
    \caption{Mean best-fitness convergence per constraint-handling strategy (30 seeds, kroA100). Repair converges to the lowest plateau, PMX overlaps closely, and the penalty arm remains elevated.}
    \label{fig:conv-strategy}
\end{figure}

\fref{fig:conv-combined} adds the mean-population and diversity panels
alongside best fitness. The diversity curve shows that all three arms lose
the bulk of their population diversity in the first 100 generations; the
repair arm retains a marginally higher floor, which is consistent with the
stochastic re-insertion step injecting variation at repaired positions.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/figures/convergence_combined.pdf}
    \caption{Three-panel convergence: best fitness (left), mean population fitness (centre), and population diversity (right) per strategy.}
    \label{fig:conv-combined}
\end{figure}

The remaining convergence figures isolate the effect of each GA parameter
swept in Part B, holding the other parameters at the best-configuration
values. Larger populations converge to a lower final fitness, with
diminishing returns past pop\_size~$=$~200. The higher mutation rate
($r_m = 0.10$) converges faster and to a lower plateau than the lower
rates, which stall earlier. Selection shows the clearest separation:
tournament dominates roulette throughout the run, with roulette plateauing
at a substantially higher fitness.

\subsection{Solution-Quality Distribution Across Runs}
\label{T4:Boxplots}

\fref{fig:boxplot-strategy} shows the distribution of final tour lengths
across the 30 seeds of each constraint-handling arm. All three arms produce
valid tours by construction (PMX preserves the permutation; repair restores
it; the penalty arm reports the penalised score, with the underlying
chromosome resolved to feasibility at evaluation time), so the visible
separation reflects actual quality differences, not infeasibility
artefacts. The repair median sits at the bottom, PMX overlaps with repair
through most of its interquartile range, and the penalty box is offset
clearly above both --- its lower whisker does not overlap the upper whiskers
of repair or PMX.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../results/figures/boxplot_strategy.pdf}
    \caption{Final tour length by constraint-handling strategy (30 runs each). Repair and PMX overlap; penalty is visibly separated upward.}
    \label{fig:boxplot-strategy}
\end{figure}

The per-parameter boxplots mirror the
sensitivity ordering visible in the convergence curves. Selection method
produces the largest box separation: the roulette and tournament boxes do
not overlap at all. Mutation rate shows a clear monotonic improvement as
$r_m$ rises from 0.01 to 0.10. Population size produces a more modest
shift, with the larger populations producing tighter as well as lower
distributions.

\subsection{Best-Tour Visualisation}
\label{T4:BestTour}

\fref{fig:best-tour} renders the best tour found across the entire sweep:
fitness 42{,}622.53, obtained from seed 16 of the best configuration
(pop\_size~$=$~200, $r_c = 0.95$, $r_m = 0.10$, tournament, PMX with
repair). Each edge is drawn with \texttt{matplotlib.pyplot.quiver} so that
the arrowhead indicates the direction in which the tour traverses the edge.
The gap from the known optimal of 21{,}282 is approximately 100.3\%.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../results/figures/best_tour.pdf}
    \caption{Best tour found on kroA100 (fitness 42{,}622.53, seed 16, best sweep configuration). Arrows drawn with \texttt{ax.quiver} show traversal direction; the gap from the optimal is 100.3\%.}
    \label{fig:best-tour}
\end{figure}

The large gap from the optimal is expected. The GA used here pairs a naive
crossover with random-insertion repair and no local-search component;
state-of-the-art TSP solvers attach a 2-opt or Lin--Kernighan step inside
the evaluation loop, which removes exactly the kind of crossing edges
visible in this figure. Looking at the tour confirms this: several
edges cross each other and several others take unnecessary detours
through a single city that a 2-opt swap would easily fix. The chosen baseline is the
assignment's specified setup, not a competitive TSP heuristic, and the gap
should be understood with this context in mind.

\subsection{Results Discussion}
\label{T4:Discussion}

Overall the results rank the three strategies as repair $\le$ PMX $<$
penalty by mean solution quality. The penalty arm levels off at a higher
fitness rather than catching up with more generations, confirming that
enforcing feasibility directly gives the GA a cleaner search signal.
Among the swept parameters, selection method has the largest effect,
followed by mutation rate and then population size, but the
best-vs-worst gap in \fref{fig:conv-best-worst} exceeds any
single-parameter effect, so parameter interactions matter as well. The
main limitation is the absolute gap from the optimal; adding a 2-opt
local search inside the GA loop or as a post-processing step would
remove the crossing edges visible in \fref{fig:best-tour} and reduce
the gap without changing the constraint-handling conclusions.
