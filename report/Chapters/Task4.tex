\subsection{Convergence Curves (Task 4.1)}
\label{T4:Convergence}

\fref{fig:conv-strategy} shows the mean best-fitness convergence curves
($\pm$~1 standard deviation) across 30 seeds for each constraint-handling
strategy from the three-arm comparative experiment. All three strategies
start from the same initial fitness level ($\approx$~151,000) because the
initial populations are generated identically before the first crossover
step.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.85\textwidth]{../results/figures/convergence_best_fitness.pdf}
    \caption{Convergence of best fitness per strategy (naive crossover, 30 seeds). Repair produces valid tours with higher but meaningful fitness. No-repair converges to lower but infeasible values. Penalty is highest because the penalty weight inflates reported fitness.}
    \label{fig:conv-strategy}
\end{figure}

The no-repair arm descends the fastest and reaches the lowest final values,
but these values represent infeasible tours that skip cities. The repair arm
converges more slowly to higher values, but every evaluated chromosome is a
valid 100-city tour. The penalty arm converges slowest and plateaus highest
because the penalty weight ($w = 50{,}000$) adds substantial cost to
infeasible chromosomes, forcing the GA to earn feasibility through selection
pressure alone.

\fref{fig:conv-combined} presents a combined three-panel view of best
fitness, mean population fitness, and population diversity over generations.
The diversity panel shows that all three strategies experience rapid
diversity loss in the first 50--100 generations, with the repair arm
retaining slightly higher final diversity (0.013 versus 0.010 for no-repair).

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{../results/figures/convergence_combined.pdf}
    \caption{Combined convergence view: best fitness (left), mean fitness (centre), and population diversity (right) for all three strategies.}
    \label{fig:conv-combined}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Solution-Quality Distribution Across Runs}
\label{T4:Boxplots}

\fref{fig:boxplot-strategy} shows the distribution of final tour lengths
across 30 runs for each constraint-handling strategy.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.8\textwidth]{../results/figures/boxplot_strategy.pdf}
    \caption{Boxplots of final tour length by constraint-handling strategy (kroA100, naive crossover, 30 runs per strategy).}
    \label{fig:boxplot-strategy}
\end{figure}

The three strategies are clearly separated. No-repair (mean 38,129) produces
the lowest recorded distances but these are not valid TSP solutions. Repair
(mean 49,778) produces meaningful tour lengths with the tightest
interquartile range of the three strategies. Penalty (mean 57,948) produces
the highest values because the penalty weight inflates fitness for
chromosomes that still contain violations.

Pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests with Bonferroni correction
($\alpha_{\text{corrected}} = 0.0167$ for three comparisons) confirm that
all three pairwise differences are statistically significant
($p < 0.001$ for all pairs). \tref{tab:wilcoxon-strategy} summarises these
results.

\begin{table}[h]
    \centering
    \caption{Pairwise Wilcoxon signed-rank tests (Bonferroni-corrected $\alpha = 0.0167$). All differences are statistically significant.}
    \label{tab:wilcoxon-strategy}
    \begin{tabular}{l l r r l}
        \hline
        Strategy 1 & Strategy 2 & Statistic & $p$-value & Lower mean \\
        \hline
        Repair & Penalty & 1.00 & $< 0.001$ & Repair \\
        Repair & No repair & 0.00 & $< 0.001$ & No repair \\
        Penalty & No repair & 0.00 & $< 0.001$ & No repair \\
        \hline
    \end{tabular}
\end{table}

The repair vs.\ penalty comparison is the most informative: repair
significantly outperforms penalty ($p < 0.001$), confirming that direct
feasibility enforcement is more effective than indirect penalty-based
steering for this problem instance and operator configuration.

\subsection{Best-Tour Visualisation}
\label{T4:BestTour}

\fref{fig:best-tour} shows the best tour found across all 30 seeds of the
best sweep configuration (pop\_size~$=$~200, $r_c$~$=$~0.95,
$r_m$~$=$~0.10, tournament, PMX with repair). The tour visits all 100
cities exactly once and returns to the starting city.

\begin{figure}[h]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=0.75\textwidth]{../results/figures/best_tour.pdf}
    \caption{Best tour found on kroA100 (tour length 37,956). Cities are plotted at their EUC\_2D coordinates with edges in visit order.}
    \label{fig:best-tour}
\end{figure}

The tour exhibits the expected structure for a near-optimal Euclidean TSP
solution: edges are predominantly short and the tour avoids large crossings,
though several crossing edges remain, indicating room for improvement via
local search operators such as 2-opt.

\subsection{Results Discussion}
\label{T4:Discussion}

The experimental results address the central research question posed in
\sref{Intro:ProblemStatement}: \emph{How does a repair mechanism affect
the quality, diversity, and convergence of a GA applied to the TSP,
compared to alternative constraint-handling strategies?}

\textbf{On solution quality.}
The repair mechanism produces valid tours whose fitness values are
meaningful and comparable. Without repair, the GA converges to solutions
with statistically lower fitness values (\tref{tab:repair-summary},
\tref{tab:wilcoxon-strategy}), but these are not valid tours --- they
visit fewer than 100 unique cities (\tref{tab:repair-sanity}). The penalty
function keeps chromosomes in the population but steers toward feasibility
indirectly, resulting in higher tour lengths than repair
(\fref{fig:boxplot-strategy}). Among the three strategies, repair is the
only one that guarantees every evaluated solution is a valid TSP tour while
also producing the lowest meaningful fitness values.

\textbf{On population diversity.}
Both repair and no-repair arms experience rapid diversity collapse in the
first 50--100 generations (\fref{fig:conv-combined}). However, repair
retains slightly higher final diversity (0.013 vs.\ 0.010) than no-repair
(\tref{tab:repair-convergence}). This is consistent with the random-insertion
repair strategy, which introduces stochastic variation at repaired positions.
The concern raised in the literature that repair might accelerate diversity
loss~\cite{padhye2015feasibilitypreserving} was not observed in this
experiment.

\textbf{On convergence behaviour.}
Repair produces a convergence curve that is initially slower than no-repair
but reaches a meaningful plateau (\fref{fig:conv-strategy}). The no-repair
arm's faster descent is misleading because it reflects convergence toward
infeasible solutions. The penalty arm converges slowest because the fitness
landscape is distorted by the additive penalty term.

\textbf{On parameter sensitivity.}
The parameter sweep (\sref{T3:Sensitivity}) shows that selection method
has the largest single-parameter effect on solution quality
(\fref{fig:boxplot-selection}), followed by mutation rate
(\fref{fig:boxplot-mutation}) and population size (\fref{fig:boxplot-pop}).
The best configuration (tournament, $r_m = 0.10$, pop\_size~$=$~200)
achieved a gap of 123.4\% from the optimal, while the worst (roulette,
$r_m = 0.05$, pop\_size~$=$~200) reached 402.4\%. This demonstrates that
GA parameter choice has a larger effect on solution quality than the gap
between the best GA result and the known optimal.

\textbf{Limitations of the current GA.}
The best single run (tour length 37,956) is still 78.4\% above the known
optimal of 21,282. This gap is expected for a plain generational GA without
local search. State-of-the-art TSP solvers combine evolutionary operators
with 2-opt or Lin--Kernighan local search~\cite{liu2014powerfulga}, which
can close much of this gap. The purpose of this project was not to match
optimal solutions but to investigate constraint-handling strategies, and the
results clearly demonstrate the qualitative differences between repair,
penalty, and no-repair approaches.

\textbf{Connection to repair mechanism analysis.}
The findings in this section are consistent with the critical analysis in
\sref{T3:RepairCritique}. The repair mechanism is necessary when using
operators that break the permutation property, effective at maintaining
feasibility without accelerating diversity loss, and preferable to the
penalty-function alternative tested. For permutation-preserving operators
(PMX, OX, CX), repair serves as a safety net rather than the primary
constraint handler.
